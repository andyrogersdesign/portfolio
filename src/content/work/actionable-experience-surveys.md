## The problem

Imagine cooking for a guest you've never met. You ask what they like, get shrugs, so you make a few small pizzas and hope. Every one comes back "lovely!". Lovely, and useless: next time you're none the wiser about what actually worked.

That's a customer satisfaction score. For a year, the learner surveys I inherited averaged 4.5 out of 5, comfortably over target. Leadership loved it. So did I. But every course and every question clustered around "lovely", telling me nothing about where I was weak or what to change next. A high score you can't act on isn't a win.

## How I framed it

The learners weren't the problem; the instrument was. The questions were leading ("the course was useful, agree?"), and the answers were mashed onto a strongly-agree-to-strongly-disagree scale, then averaged into one figure. Meaning leaks out at every step. This is the core of Will Thalheimer's work on learner surveys: agreement scales are ambiguous, tiring, and correlate only weakly with what people actually learn. So I stopped chasing a better score and started designing for a better answer.

## What I tried

I rebuilt every question around three moves:

- **Neutral, open questions** instead of leading statements.
- **Specific, real-world answer options** instead of a 1–5 scale — four or so sentences a real person might say, each concrete enough to act on.
- **A four-level standard** (Alarming, Concerning, Acceptable, Superior) behind each option, so one question yields quantitative benchmarking *and* qualitative diagnosis at once.

Two behavioural-science effects made it work. The format forces **metacognition** — people genuinely appraise their experience rather than coast down a column — and it **primes** the free-text box that follows, so comments come out focused instead of vague.

## The result

Same people, same goodwill, but now with edges I could act on. On one course, a strong majority (**64%**) said the practice examples had improved their actual work, while a smaller share pointed to specific fixes, such as the **12%** who simply wanted more. Elsewhere, asking how people *actually* use the guidelines surfaced that around **one in five** were relying on printed copies that may already be out of date — a behavioural risk a satisfaction score would have buried entirely.

## What you can take away

- A high score isn't the goal; actionable information is. If feedback can't tell you what to do next, its number doesn't matter.
- Replace agreement scales with specific, behaviour-anchored options — you get counting and diagnosis from a single question.
- Ask the hard question *before* the comment box; the thinking you provoke is the thinking you'll read back.
- It isn't a cure-all: for a quick demographic fact, a plain question still wins. Reach for this when the answer has to tell you what to do next.

The deeper point every corporate educator wrestles with: you're forever asked to reduce something rich and human to a number, as if you can have the number or the meaning but not both. That's a false choice. Design the questions well enough, and the number stops standing in for the meaning and starts carrying it. None of this is really about training, either — the same redesign works for CSAT, NPS, product research, employee engagement, and any survey averaging opinions into a hollow figure.
